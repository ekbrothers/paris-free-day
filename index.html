Before you go -- must-knows

🎟

Book tickets before you leave home

The Louvre, Musee d'Orsay, Musee de l'Orangerie, and Marmottan Monet all require timed-entry tickets. Walk-up lines at the Louvre can exceed an hour. Book online at least 2 weeks in advance.

💳

Get a Navigo Easy card

Paper metro tickets no longer exist as of 2025. Get a Navigo Easy card at any metro station for €2, then load single rides at €2.55 each. Every attraction on this itinerary is reachable on the Metro.

🕘

Arrive at museums early

The Orangerie and Louvre are best before 10am. Marmottan is much smaller and quieter throughout the day. Tour groups flood central museums mid-morning.

👟

Wear very comfortable shoes

The Louvre alone has miles of galleries. Montmartre is cobblestoned and hilly. Expect 6--10 miles of walking across Day 4 and Day 5.

📱

Download Google Maps offline

Download the Paris map area before your trip. Metro navigation is much easier with a visual map, especially when switching lines underground.

👜

Watch for pickpockets

Metro Lines 1 and 4 and busy stations like Chatelet are common pickpocket spots. Keep bags zipped and in front of you. This is common and manageable with awareness.

Day 2 — Thursday, September 17

Free morning + Welcome dinner

A relaxed first full day -- one exceptional museum off the tourist track, then the tour's welcome dinner in the evening.

Welcome dinner timing The welcome dinner is expected around 5--6pm. Plan to be back at the hotel and ready by 4:30pm at the latest. This gives you a comfortable morning window from 9am to roughly 3pm.
9:00 am
Musee Marmottan Monet Priority

The largest Monet collection in the world, housed in a 19th-century hunting lodge in the Bois de Boulogne. Unlike the Louvre or Orsay, this is an intimate museum -- no crowds, no rush. The collection includes over 300 Monet works, including the original Impression, Sunrise painting that gave Impressionism its name. Plan 1.5--2 hours. Open Tuesday through Sunday, 10am--6pm (until 9pm on Thursdays). Closed Mondays.

🚇 Metro Line 9 to La Muette -- 5-min walk through the Ranelagh garden to the museum
⏱ ~25--30 min from central Paris (Chatelet area)
11:30 am
Trocadero + Eiffel Tower views

A 15-minute walk or one Metro stop from Marmottan brings you to the Trocadero esplanade -- the best unobstructed view of the Eiffel Tower in Paris, and completely free. A natural first-day-in-Paris moment. The tower is right across the Seine; you can walk across Pont d'Iena to get close if you like.

🚇 Metro Line 9 to Trocadero (one stop from La Muette), or walk ~15 min through Passy
12:30 pm
Lunch in Passy / 16th

The Passy neighborhood around the Trocadero has excellent cafes and brasseries -- quieter and more local-feeling than the tourist zones. Take your time. You have a comfortable window before needing to head back.

🚶 Walking distance from Trocadero
~3:00 pm
Return to hotel

Head back with time to freshen up before the welcome dinner. The Metro ride back to central Paris is easy from Trocadero or La Muette.

🚇 Metro Line 9 from Trocadero or La Muette toward central Paris
5:00 pm
Welcome dinner Trafalgar tour

Included with the tour. A chance to meet your fellow travelers and Travel Director before the Paris days begin in earnest.

Day 4 — Saturday, September 19

Museums + Left Bank

A full day along the Seine -- three world-class museums, a riverside walk, and dinner at your own pace.

9:00 am
Musee de l'Orangerie Priority

Start here. The two oval rooms of Monet's Water Lilies are among the most peaceful experiences in Paris, and they're quietest at opening. Plan 1.5 hours. The museum is compact and unhurried -- perfect for a relaxed pace. Open Wednesday through Monday, 9am--6pm. Closed Tuesdays. Saturday is a busy day in Paris -- arriving at opening makes a real difference.

🚇 Metro Line 1 to Concorde, 3-min walk into Tuileries Garden
10:30 am
Tuileries Garden walk

Exit the Orangerie and walk east through the Tuileries toward the Louvre -- about a 10-minute stroll. Fountains, open sky, and a good spot to grab a pastry or coffee from the garden kiosk before the museum. No ticket needed, just walk through.

🚶 On foot, ~10 min from Orangerie to Louvre entrance
11:00 am
The Louvre Priority

Budget 2.5--3 hours for highlights at a relaxed pace: the Mona Lisa, Venus de Milo, and Winged Victory of Samothrace. Saturdays are busy -- enter via the Passage Richelieu (not the Pyramid) if you already have a ticket, as it's significantly faster. The Louvre is open until 6pm on Saturdays.

🚶 Already on foot from Tuileries Garden
Louvre tip The museum is enormous -- do not try to see it all. Pick 3--4 rooms and enjoy them properly. A rushed 5-hour sprint will leave you exhausted. The Richelieu wing (French sculpture and Northern European painting) is often less crowded than the Denon wing where the Mona Lisa lives.
2:00 pm
Lunch near Saint-Germain-des-Pres

Cross Pont des Arts (the "love lock bridge") over the Seine to the Left Bank. The streets around Rue de Buci and Boulevard Saint-Germain are full of excellent, unfussy cafes. Order whatever looks good -- there is no wrong answer in this neighborhood. Plan 45--60 minutes.

🚶 On foot across Pont des Arts, ~12 min from Louvre exit
3:30 pm
Musee d'Orsay Priority

A 15-minute walk west along the Left Bank riverfront from Saint-Germain. Saturday afternoons at the Orsay are busy but manageable if you have a timed ticket. Budget 1.5--2 hours for the Impressionist collection on the top floor: Monet, Renoir, Degas, Van Gogh. The building itself -- a converted 1900 railway station -- is worth admiring. Open until 6pm.

🚶 On foot ~15 min west along the Seine from Saint-Germain
🚇 Metro Line 12 to Solferino, or RER C to Musee d'Orsay
6:00 pm+
Evening at leisure

Dinner on the Left Bank at your own pace. A walk back along the Seine at dusk, with the city lighting up, is one of the best free things Paris offers. No agenda -- rest up for Montmartre tomorrow.

🚇 RER C or Metro Line 12 back toward central Paris
Booking required for Day 4 Buy timed-entry tickets for the Orangerie, Louvre, and Orsay before your trip. See the booking links section at the bottom of this page. Saturday is the busiest day of the week -- do not skip pre-booking.

Day 5 — Sunday, September 20

Montmartre morning + Tour afternoon

Wander the bohemian hilltop village that inspired generations of painters, then meet the tour for Giverny and the Seine cruise.

Important -- confirm tour pick-up time The Giverny and Seine River Cruise excursions are through the Trafalgar tour. Check with your Travel Director for the exact departure time. That tells you how much morning time you actually have. The morning plan below assumes you have until at least noon.
9:00 am
Montmartre neighborhood Priority

Take the Metro to Abbesses station (Line 12) -- it puts you right in the heart of Montmartre without a long climb. Wander the cobblestone streets, peek into galleries, and find a cafe for a second coffee. This neighborhood rewards a slow wander far more than a checklist approach. Sunday morning is one of the nicest times to be here.

🚇 Metro Line 12 to Abbesses -- direct, ~15--20 min from central Paris
🚇 Or Line 2 to Anvers, then walk up or take the free funicular
10:00 am
Sacre Coeur Basilica Priority

Free entry. The view from the front steps over Paris is one of the best in the city. Arrive by 10am on a Sunday to beat the largest tour groups. The interior is beautiful -- white stone, mosaics, candles. Plan 30--45 minutes. Walk back down the hill through the vineyard streets afterward.

🚡 Free funicular from the bottom of the hill (uses a regular metro ticket)
🚶 Or walk up the Rue Foyatier steps -- steeper but scenic
11:30 am
Notre Dame Cathedral Priority

Fully restored and reopened after the 2019 fire. Take the Metro from Montmartre down to the Ile de la Cite. The exterior alone is breathtaking -- but go inside if time allows. A timed reservation is now required; book in advance on the official site. Plan 45--60 minutes. Entry is free.

🚇 Metro Line 4 from Chateau Rouge (Montmartre) to Cite -- about 13 min, direct
1:00 pm
Lunch on Ile Saint-Louis

Cross the small footbridge from Notre Dame to Ile Saint-Louis -- a quieter, charming island with excellent cafes and the legendary Berthillon ice cream shop. A good midday rest before tour activities begin.

🚶 On foot from Notre Dame, 2-min walk across Pont Saint-Louis
Afternoon
Giverny + Seine River Cruise Trafalgar tour

Both are already included through the Trafalgar tour. The Giverny excursion visits Monet's actual home and gardens -- the same gardens that inspired the Water Lilies you saw at the Orangerie on Day 4, and the paintings you saw at Marmottan on Day 2. The Seine cruise offers the monuments of Paris from the water at dusk.

🚌 Tour transport provided -- confirm pick-up point and time with your Travel Director

Getting around Paris

The Metro is your best friend

A single ride costs €2.55 and gets you anywhere in central Paris. The Metro runs from about 5:30am to 1am (2:15am on Friday and Saturday nights).

Most important tip Get a Navigo Easy card (€2 one-time) at any metro station ticket machine. Paper tickets no longer exist. Load single rides onto the card. If you are staying all week, a weekly Navigo pass (Monday--Sunday) may be better value if it covers your dates.

Key lines for this itinerary

  • Line 9: La Muette (Marmottan Monet), Trocadero
  • Line 1 (east-west): Louvre-Rivoli, Concorde (Orangerie / Tuileries)
  • Line 12: Abbesses (Montmartre), Solferino (near Orsay)
  • Line 4: Cite (Notre Dame), Chateau Rouge (Montmartre)
  • Line 6: Bir-Hakeim (Eiffel Tower)
  • RER C: Musee d'Orsay stop (direct), Champ de Mars-Tour Eiffel

Walking is often faster than you expect

The cluster of museums on Day 4 is very walkable:

  • Orangerie to Louvre on foot: ~10 min through Tuileries Garden
  • Louvre to Musee d'Orsay on foot: ~20 min along the river
  • Notre Dame to Montmartre by Metro: ~13 min (Line 4)
  • Marmottan to Trocadero on foot: ~15 min through Passy

Taxis and Uber

Both work in Paris. Uber is called Uber and functions the same as home. Useful late at night or if your feet give out. Central Paris traffic is often slow, so the Metro is usually faster for short trips during the day.

Gotchas to avoid

  • Marmottan Monet is closed Mondays -- your visit is on a Thursday, which is ideal (the museum stays open until 9pm on Thursdays). No issues with your dates.
  • The Orangerie is closed Tuesdays -- your visit is on a Saturday. No issues.
  • The Louvre is closed Tuesdays -- your visit is on a Saturday. No issues.
  • Saturday is the busiest day for Paris museums -- pre-booking timed-entry tickets for the Orangerie, Louvre, and Orsay is not optional on Day 4. Walk-up queues on Saturdays in September are long.
  • Notre Dame now requires a timed reservation -- entry is free but you must book a time slot on the official website in advance. Walk-up entry is not guaranteed.
  • The Eiffel Tower summit sells out weeks ahead -- if you want to go up, book online now. Same-day tickets are essentially unavailable in September.
  • The Passage Richelieu at the Louvre is faster than the Pyramid -- use it if you already have a ticket. The Pyramid entrance has long security queues even with a reservation.
  • Versailles is crossed off for a reason -- you noted "no Versailles." Feel free to skip it entirely.
  • Giverny scheduling note: the tour documents say the Giverny excursion "may be offered on Day 5 or Day 6 depending on the day of the week." Confirm the exact day with your Travel Director so Day 5's morning plan is calibrated correctly.
  • Paper metro tickets no longer exist -- any old Paris travel advice referencing a "carnet of 10" or paper tickets is outdated. Get the Navigo Easy card.
